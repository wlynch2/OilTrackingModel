For this project we examined whether naval traffic in the Persian Gulf can be used to predict daily oil prices, with a focus on the Strait of Hormuz, which is one of the most important oil transit chokepoints in the world especially now. Because fluctuations in tanker movement often reflect geopolitical tension or supply uncertainty, our group set out to determine whether these patterns are useful for predictive value for oil price forecasting. To define this relationship, we developed and compared two models, a Linear Regression model and an LSTM neural network. The goal was to evaluate which approach better captures the connection between tanker traffic and oil prices and to determine whether naval activity provides meaningful predictive power. We began by collecting two primary datasets. The first dataset consisted of daily oil tanker counts scraped from publicly available AIS data. We filtered the AIS records to include only oil tankers passing through the Strait of Hormuz, removed duplicate ship entries, cleaned unnecessary metadata, and restricted the dataset to dates after January 1, 2019 because older AIS logs contained formatting inconsistencies. The second dataset consisted of daily WTI Spot Price data, which we selected as our benchmark because the project focuses on the United States market. We added a Price Observation flag to indicate whether a real market price existed on each date and forward filled missing weekend and holiday prices to avoid creating artificial price movements. After cleaning and aligning both datasets, we merged them into a unified time series containing approximately 2,145 days of observations. Once the data was collected, we prepared it for modeling by standardizing AIS fields, combining tanker counts into daily totals, and normalizing features for the LSTM model. We created sliding windows of seven day sequences so the LSTM could learn temporal patterns, using the past seven days of oil prices and tanker counts to predict the eighth day. The dataset was split chronologically into an 80 percent training set and a 20 percent testing set to prevent data leakage and ensure realistic evaluation.
For model selection, we chose Linear Regression as a simple and interpretable baseline to test whether a direct linear relationship exists between tanker traffic and oil prices. In contrast, the LSTM model was selected because it is designed for sequential data and can capture lag effects, nonlinear relationships, and dependencies that Linear Regression cannot. This dual model approach allowed us to compare simplicity and interpretability with the more advanced aspects of a deep learning model.
Training the models revealed clear differences in their performance. The Linear Regression model trained quickly and provided insight into general trends, but it struggled with volatility and could not account for time dependent patterns. The LSTM model required more careful tuning. We trained it using the Adam optimizer and 300 epochs with a learning rate of 0.005 and added a 20 percent dropout layer after early experiments showed signs of overfitting. At first, the LSTM appeared to memorize the training data, performing extremely well on training data but poorly on testing data. Adding dropout forced the model to ignore a portion of neurons during training, which improved generalization. Through trial and error, we found that the chosen learning rate and dropout combination produced the best balance between stability and accuracy. The final LSTM configuration successfully learned meaningful patterns without overfitting.
Evaluation and testing were conducted using R squared metrics, along with chart comparisons of predicted versus actual oil prices. The Linear Regression model captured broad trends but failed to model turning points or sudden price movements. The LSTM model consistently predicted oil prices within approximately two to three dollars of actual values on the test set. It also performed well when tested on user input scenarios, producing stable and realistic outputs. These results show that the LSTM model is far better suited for this type of time series forecasting because it can recognize how tanker traffic and oil prices influence each other over time.
Our findings indicate that naval traffic in the Persian Gulf does contain meaningful predictive signals for oil prices. The Strait of Hormuz plays a central role in global oil transportation, and tanker counts reflect both supply conditions and geopolitical risk. The LSTM model’s strong performance highlights the importance of temporal modeling in economic forecasting, especially when one variable influences another over time. However, the project also has limitations. AIS data can be noisy or incomplete, and many external factors such as OPEC decisions, sanctions, wars, and global demand shifts also affect oil prices but were not included in our model. A larger dataset and additional features could further improve accuracy, although care must be taken to avoid overfitting.

While Linear Regression provides a simple baseline, the LSTM model proved significantly more effective, achieving predictions within a narrow error range and capturing complex temporal relationships. With additional data and expanded features, the predictive power of this system could be further enhanced, offering valuable insights for analysts, economists, and organizations monitoring global oil markets.
